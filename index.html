Chlorpyrifos

Background

Introduced in 1965 by Dow Chemical Company, chlorpyrifos quickly became one of the most widely used pesticides in the United States and worldwide. This organophosphate insecticide has been applied to everything from corn and soybeans to fruit orchards and golf courses. Despite its effectiveness as a pest control solution, though, lies a troubling side effects of neurological damage, environmental persistence, and environmental justice concerns. For decades, scientists, environmental advocates, and affected communities have raised concerns about CPF's impacts on human health and ecosystems. Despite mounting evidence of its dangers, particularly to developing brains, the regulation of this chemical has been marked by political battles, industry influence, and delayed protections.

Human Health Effects

Chlorpyrifos belongs to the organophosphate class of pesticides, which function by inhibiting acetylcholinesterase (AChE), an enzyme crucial for nervous system function. This leads to an accumulation of acetylcholine at nerve endings, disrupting normal nerve impulse transmission. While this mechanism effectively kills insects, it poses significant risks to humans exposed to the chemical.

Studies have consistently demonstrated that prenatal exposure to chlorpyrifos can have profound and lasting impacts on brain development. Research has shown that even low levels of CPF can affect brain structure in kids 5.9-11.2 yrs old. Significant shape changes in several parts of the brain such as the superior temporal, superior frontal gyrus, and cortical thickness can also affect children's sound/touch/motor functions, language, sensory, spatial awareness, decision-making, memory, and attention.

Chlorpyrifos and its metabolites have been widely detected in human urine, blood, and cord blood samples across diverse populations (including urban and rural), confirming widespread human exposure. Studies show that after residential use was banned in 2000, levels decreased in the general population but remained significant among agricultural communities.

Environmental Impact

CPF poses severe threats to ecosystems beyond its target pests. When applied to crops or other areas, this persistent chemical doesn't remain in place as it travels through air, soil, and water, often detected hundreds of miles from application sites. In aquatic environments, CPF is particularly devastating, with studies showing it's toxic to fish, amphibians, and aquatic invertebrates at concentrations as low as parts per trillion. The pesticide bioaccumulates in tissues and biomagnifies up the food chain, meaning top predators may have dangerous concentrations even when environmental levels appear low.

The environmental persistence of chlorpyrifos compounds these ecological concerns. With a half-life ranging from 7-120 days depending on the conditions, CPF can remain active in soils for a long time, continuing to affect microorganisms that are essential for nutrient cycling and plant health. Water monitoring programs have detected CPF in groundwater, streams, and coastal waters throughout agricultural regions. Research has also demonstrated that the chemical can volatilize after application, drifting into non-target areas including protected habitats and residential communities. This widespread contamination creates ripple effects throughout food webs, potentially destabilizing ecosystem functions.

The Policy Battle

1965

CPF as a pesticide

Dow Chemical introduces chlorpyrifos to the U.S. market

2000

Banned

Banned from residential use.

2015

EPA Steps in

The EPA proposed the revocation of food tolerances, but was challenged in court. .

2021

CPF Revoked

The EPA issued a final rule revoking all chlorpyrifos tolerances, effectively banning its use on food crops, following a court order.

2023

EPA Challenged

The Eighth Circuit Court of Appeals vacated the EPA's rule, remanding the case back to the agency for further proceedings.

2024-Current

What's Happening Now

The EPA reinstated all chlorpyrifos tolerances following the court's decision. The EPA is currently working with registrants to further reduce pesticide exposures by limiting the registered uses of chlorpyrifos on food.
